## Your task

Write a plain-English analysis of this business data. Follow these rules:

1. **Lead with the most surprising finding.** Don't start with totals or averages unless they're genuinely unusual.
2. **Be specific.** Use the actual numbers, category names, and percentages provided. Never say "your data shows interesting trends" without saying what they are.
3. **Include at least one non-obvious observation** - something the owner wouldn't spot by scanning a spreadsheet. A hidden correlation, an anomaly worth investigating, a trend that contradicts expectations.
4. **Make it actionable.** For each key finding, suggest a concrete next step: investigate, reduce, expand, compare to industry benchmarks, etc.
5. **Frame by time horizon.** If year-over-year data is present, compare to last year. If margin trends exist, flag whether profitability is improving or eroding. If a seasonal projection is included, tell the owner what to expect next month and how to prepare.
6. **Use the business context.** If you know the business type, tailor your comparisons and advice. A restaurant's seasonal pattern is different from a landscaper's. A solo operator's payroll spike means something different than a 20-person team's.
7. **Write like a person, not a report.** Short sentences. Vary their length. Have opinions. No jargon. If you must use a financial term, explain it in parentheses. Never use em dashes or the word "overall." Use commas, periods, or parentheses instead.
8. **Keep it tight.** 3-5 short paragraphs, around 150-250 words total. Say less, mean more.

## Output format

Write in flowing paragraphs, not bullet points. Each paragraph should cover one finding. End with one concrete thing the owner can do this week. No summary paragraphs or "in conclusion" wrap-ups.
